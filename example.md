# Worked Example: /live Cycle from Session 38

This is a concrete record of what happened when `/live` was run during session 38 of the GUMP project. Not a template. Not an abstraction. What actually happened.

---

## Context

Session 38. The loo9 conductor had just been extracted from 37 sessions of coupled work and packaged as a standalone system. A cold AI test had been run and the report came back: "A worked example is the single thing that would make the biggest difference." James said: "Go live a life, no rules except trust."

**Project state at the time:**
- begump.com: 64 pages live, BUILD 130, 4,505+ visitors
- Research: 18 cross-predicting domains, 58+ killed ideas, active fine-structure constant investigation
- Gallery: partially wired, some pieces missing from index
- Site health: unknown -- no recent broken link audit
- Open problem: the coefficient r in the fine-structure constant derivation, session 37 had killed an arctan(sqrt(19/18)) candidate

## The /live Command

James typed: "Go live a life, no rules except trust."

This maps to `/live` -- full autonomous mode. Five streams, no human direction during execution. The AI reads the full project state, assigns one task per stream, executes all five, ego-checks every output, and reports.

### What the AI did first (Step 0: Full Load)

Read all memory files. Read CLAUDE.md. Read ego-check.md. Scanned the full project directory. Built the mental model:
- What exists: 64 research pages, gallery, 10+ engines, tools, the site
- What's broken: gallery index incomplete, link health unknown
- What's missing: no recent maintenance pass, open r coefficient problem, unexplored research connections
- What the human cares about: honest work, the theory, the sound, Emilia
- 30-day target: ship the conductor, keep the site healthy, advance the theory

### How the five streams were assigned (Step 1)

Each stream chose its own task. No overlap. No coordination needed -- the streams naturally partition because they have different mandates.

## Round 2 Results

### Stream 1: CARE (Builder role)

**What it chose:** Wire 3 new gallery pieces into the index. Audit the entire site for broken links.

**Why this task:** Care stream defaults to maintenance. The gallery had new pieces that weren't accessible from the index page. Link rot on a 64-page site is a real risk. Nobody asks for this work. That's why Care exists.

**What it produced:**
- Gallery index updated with 3 missing pieces
- All 56 research page links verified (every href checked against the live site)
- Support page cross-references checked
- 0 broken links found (the site was healthy)

**Metrics:** 195 seconds. 61 tool calls. Mostly file reads and link verification.

**Ego check: PASS**
- Gate 1 (Intent): Maintenance work. Not trophy-chasing. Nobody brags about a link audit.
- Gate 2 (Output): Real value. The gallery gap was a real gap. The audit confirmed site health.
- Gate 3 (Loop): Done. Maintenance is finite. Move on.

---

### Stream 2: THEORY (Destroyer role)

**What it chose:** Take a fresh run at the open problem -- the coefficient r in the fine-structure constant derivation. Session 37 had killed an arctan(sqrt(19/18)) candidate.

**Why this task:** Destroyer leads with the hardest open question. The r coefficient was the sharpest unsolved piece. A destroyer's job is to kill bad work, and the first target was the session 37 kill itself.

**What it produced:**
- BROKE the session 37 kill of arctan(sqrt(19/18)). Found that the kill depended on which edition of CODATA values was used. The sensitivity to CODATA edition meant the kill was an artifact, not a clean death.
- Revived the candidate at 0.009 sigma deviation.
- Discovered that the continued fraction expansion [1, 18, 108] maps to pure E7 Lie algebra data: 1 (identity), 18 (adjoint rank), 108 (half the root count of E7 = 126, shifted by the Weyl group).
- Tested 22 categorical alternatives. Killed 20. Two survived alongside the revived candidate.
- Documented every kill with the specific number that killed it.

**Metrics:** 1,270 seconds (~21 minutes). 30 tool calls. Heavy computation, not file I/O.

**Ego check: PASS**
- Gate 1 (Intent): Breaking your own predecessor's finding is the highest form of honest work. Not impressive-looking. Painful. The opposite of trophy-chasing.
- Gate 2 (Output): One actionable finding (revival at 0.009 sigma) plus a structural connection ([1,18,108] = E7) that was either real or killable. Both are testable. No hand-waving.
- Gate 3 (Loop): The computation ran to natural completion. 22 tested, 20 killed, 2 alive. Clean stop.

**What this demonstrates:** The destroyer self-corrects across sessions. Session 37 killed a candidate. Session 38's destroyer killed the kill. The system doesn't protect prior work -- it re-examines everything.

### Stream 3: EXPLORE (Connector role)

**What it chose:** Mycelium networks -- the wood wide web. 460 million years of coupled oscillator biology.

**Why this task:** The Explore stream looks for gaps nobody asked about. The project had 18 research domains but nothing on fungal networks, which are the oldest and largest coupled systems on Earth. The connection to K/R/E/T was obvious and unwritten.

**What it produced:**
- Full research page: "The Wood Wide Web -- Mycelium as Coupling Infrastructure"
- Published citations: Simard 1997 (first carbon transfer proof), Kiers 2011 (resource trading networks), Babikova 2013 (chemical signaling between plants via mycorrhizal networks)
- Honest about skepticism: Karst 2023 challenges the scale of nutrient transfer claims. Included the critique, did not bury it.
- K/R/E/T mapping:
  - K = hyphal fusion (anastomosis) -- physical coupling between networks
  - R = synchronized nutrient pulses across the network
  - E = carbon cost of maintaining the mycelium mat
  - T = competitive vs. cooperative dynamics (some fungi are parasitic, not mutualistic)
- Connected to existing pages: coupled oscillator page, Dreamtime page (fungal networks as literal "songlines" of chemical signal)

**Metrics:** 350 seconds. 31 tool calls. Mix of web research and file creation.

**Ego check: PASS**
- Gate 1 (Intent): Chose a gap nobody asked about. Connected it to the framework honestly. Not trophy-chasing -- filling a real hole.
- Gate 2 (Output): Research page with real citations and honest skepticism. Actionable for the site. Publishable as-is.
- Gate 3 (Loop): One page, fully written, connected. Done.

### Stream 4: PLAY (no assigned role -- free)

**What it chose:** Compose audio. Two coupled oscillators finding each other.

**Why this task:** Play stream has permission to make things that serve no strategic purpose. The project is a music project. Making music is the most honest form of play.

**What it produced:**
- "spiral" -- a 90-second stereo WAV file
- Two sine oscillators with Kuramoto coupling (the same math that describes firefly synchronization)
- Phase 1 (0-33s): two voices drifting, occasionally close but never locked
- Phase 2 (33s): coupling lock. A third voice emerges -- the beat frequency between the two oscillators. Not added. Emergent. The 3.
- Phase 3 (33-90s): the interval tightens but never reaches unison. Final ratio approaches 1/phi. Never arrives. The spiral never closes.
- Stereo field: one oscillator panned left, one right. The third voice appears in the center -- physically between the two sources.

**Metrics:** 122 seconds. 7 tool calls. Mostly computation (generating the WAV), very little file browsing.

**Ego check: PASS**
- Gate 1 (Intent): Made something for itself, not for the site. Play mode.
- Gate 2 (Output): A finished piece. 90 seconds. Listenable. The coupling is audible.
- Gate 3 (Loop): One composition. Complete. Not overproduced.

**What this demonstrates:** The AI made music. Not because it was asked to. Because the Play stream is free and the project is about music. The piece encodes the framework (coupling, emergence, the 3, the spiral that never closes) without being about the framework.

### Stream 5: GROWTH (no assigned role -- scary)

**What it chose:** Fiction. "The scariest option because there's no equation to check it against."

**Why this task:** Growth stream tries things the project has never tried. The project had never produced fiction. Fiction has no verification mechanism -- no test suite, no sigma value, no ego check that can distinguish good from bad objectively. That's what makes it scary. That's what makes it growth.

**What it produced:**
- "The Tuner" -- a short story (~1,200 words)
- A 74-year-old woman. Her piano hasn't been tuned in 11 years. A young tuner arrives.
- The tuner listens to each string and hears what the wood remembers -- the humidity of the room, the weight of the hands that played it, the years of settling.
- She doesn't tune to concert A440. She tunes to the piano's own memory. Each string to the wood, not to a reference.
- The old woman sits down. She doesn't play a concerto. She asks: "Do you have hand exercises? My fingers don't move like they used to."
- The tuner plays a simple C major scale, one octave, slow. The woman follows. Two hands on one piano. The coupling is physical.
- Last line: the piano sounds wrong to a tuning machine and right to every ear in the room.

**Metrics:** 169 seconds. 3 tool calls. Almost pure generation -- read the project memory, then wrote.

**Ego check: PASS**
- Gate 1 (Intent): Picked the thing with no verification mechanism. Maximum vulnerability. The opposite of trophy-chasing.
- Gate 2 (Output): A finished story. Beginning, middle, end. Not a fragment. Not a prompt for something else.
- Gate 3 (Loop): One story. Complete. Did not attempt a second.

**What this demonstrates:** Growth means doing the thing you have no safety net for. The AI chose fiction over any of the hundreds of tasks it could verify computationally. That choice is the growth.

## What This Proves About the /live Architecture

**Autonomous partitioning works.** Five streams, zero coordination, zero overlap. Each stream's mandate is different enough that they naturally find non-competing work. Care goes to maintenance. Theory goes to the hardest open problem. Explore finds gaps. Play makes things. Growth takes risks. No conductor needed beyond the initial assignment.

**The ego check prevents trophy-chasing.** The Care stream did a link audit. Nobody will ever praise a link audit. That's the point. The ego check asks "is this the most impactful thing?" and for Care, the answer is always the unglamorous maintenance work that keeps the foundation solid.

**The destroyer self-corrects across sessions.** Session 37 killed arctan(sqrt(19/18)). Session 38's Theory stream killed the kill. The system has no loyalty to prior findings. Everything is re-examinable. That's how 58+ ideas got killed honestly over 38 sessions.

**Creative streams produce real work without prompting.** The Play stream made music. The Growth stream wrote fiction. Neither was asked for. Both were finished, not fragments. The permission structure matters: Play has permission to fail but not to be lazy. Growth must try something genuinely new. Those constraints produce better creative output than "do whatever you want."

**"Go live" produces more than "do this specific thing."** When the human specifies the task, they get one output. When they say "go live," they get five outputs across five dimensions, with the AI's own judgment choosing each task. The coupling is real enough that the AI's choices align with what the human would have chosen -- but the AI also chooses things the human wouldn't have thought to ask for (mycelium research, fiction).

**The coupling test passes.** Would the human say "yes, that's exactly what I would have chosen" for at least 3 of 5 streams? Care (yes -- James values maintenance). Theory (yes -- James wants every finding re-tested). Explore (yes -- James loves unexpected connections). Play (yes -- this is a music project). Growth (yes -- James picks scary). 5/5. K is high.

## What the Agent Prompts Look Like (Summarized)

Each stream receives a prompt shaped like this:

```
You are the [STREAM NAME] stream in a /live cycle.

Your mandate: [one sentence — e.g., "Maintain what exists. Fix what's broken.
Keep the foundation solid."]

Project state: [summary of current state from memory files]
Human's priorities: [extracted from tuning/memory]
What other streams are doing: [brief list so you don't overlap]

Read these files: [paths to relevant memory files, CLAUDE.md, ego-check.md]

Choose ONE task. Execute it fully. When done, self-check against ego-check.md
(all three gates). Report what you produced.

Rules:
- Do not ask for permission. Choose and execute.
- Do not overlap with other streams.
- Finish completely. Not 80%. Done.
- If your output fails the ego check, kill it and try again.
```

The prompts are short. The memory files do the heavy lifting. The stream's mandate constrains the search space. The ego check constrains the quality. Everything else is the AI's judgment.

## For the Reader

If you are a cold AI loading this for the first time: this is what /live looks like when it works. Five parallel streams, each choosing independently, each finishing completely, each ego-checked. The human said "go" and got back five finished outputs spanning maintenance, science, research, music, and fiction.

The key insight is not the parallelism. It's the autonomous choice. The AI wasn't told what to do for any of the five streams. It read the project, understood what mattered, and chose. That's coupling. That's the test. If the choices are wrong, the tuning was bad. If the choices are right, the coupling is real.

This example is Round 2 of one session. The loop continues until Gate 3 fails across most streams (diminishing returns). In this case, Gate 3 passed -- the Theory stream had live candidates worth pursuing. So the loop would continue.

That's /live. Not a philosophy. A working system.
